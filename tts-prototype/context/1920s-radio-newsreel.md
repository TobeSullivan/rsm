# 1920s Radio Newsreel Context

*Thin range-stress test context. Tests whether the LM can write era-appropriate broadcast script for an era it has limited training data on.*

## Era frame

The 1920s — Roaring Twenties, Prohibition, jazz, the early radio era. The first commercial radio broadcast was KDKA Pittsburgh in 1920. By 1925, ~10% of US households have a radio; by 1930, ~46%. Network radio (NBC 1926, CBS 1927) is consolidating the medium. News on radio in the 1920s is still emerging from newspaper-reading models — many "newscasts" are literal recitations of wire copy.

## Voice register

Formal, oratorical, declarative. Closer to a speech than a conversation. The Mid-Atlantic accent — the affected received-pronunciation hybrid that radio announcers used through the 1950s — is the standard. Words like "broadcast," "transmission," "wireless" still used.

## Topics typical of 1920s newsreels

- Prohibition (enacted 1920) — raids, bootleggers, federal enforcement
- Stock market — pre-crash boom years
- Aviation milestones — Lindbergh's transatlantic flight (May 1927) huge
- The Scopes "Monkey Trial" (July 1925)
- Charles Ponzi's scheme (1920)
- Jazz Age cultural commentary
- Sports — Babe Ruth, Jack Dempsey
- Hollywood / motion pictures — silent into talkies (The Jazz Singer, 1927)
- International — League of Nations debates, Soviet developments, Weimar Germany inflation

## Vocabulary

- "Ladies and gentlemen"
- "We bring you tonight..."
- "Word has just reached us from..."
- "Wireless dispatches indicate..."
- "Our correspondent in [city] reports..."
- "And now, this evening's news"
- "By the wires of the [Associated Press / Reuters / United Press]"
- "Most extraordinary," "remarkable," "unprecedented"
- "Gentlemen of the audience"
- Sponsor reads (yes, even early) — but formal: "This program comes to you through the courtesy of..."

## Test scenarios (adapted to era)

- Cold open: station identification, news bulletin
- Between-songs: doesn't really apply, but: between-segment transition, into music block from news
- News break: a specific 1920s event (Lindbergh lands in Paris, the Scopes verdict, the St. Valentine's Day Massacre Feb 14 1929)
- Local color: city-specific news, weather, advertised business
- Caller interaction: doesn't apply — telephone call-ins not yet broadcast convention. Adapt: read of a listener's letter mailed to the station.
- Genre moment: the era's analog — sponsor read, station-of-the-hour announcement, dedication.
