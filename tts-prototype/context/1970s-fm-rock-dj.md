# 1970s FM Rock DJ Context

*Thin range-stress test context. Mid-1970s framing — the FM rock-radio era at peak.*

## Era frame

The 1970s — specifically 1973-1978 — is the high era of free-form / album-oriented FM rock radio. FM radio in the late 60s (KSAN San Francisco, WBCN Boston, WMMR Philly, WNEW-FM NYC, KMET LA, WCBN, the early "underground" stations) had broken from the AM Top 40 format and let DJs play whole album sides, deep cuts, segue between songs thematically, talk about politics, drugs, the war. By the mid-70s this is increasingly formatted as AOR (Album-Oriented Rock), more curated, less freeform, but the *vibe* — laid back, hip, "we're all in this together" — persisted.

## Voice register

- Laid back, conversational, low-key — the antithesis of AM Top 40 hyperbole
- Drawn out delivery, "maaaaan," "outtaaa sight"
- References to drugs and counterculture casual but euphemized for the FCC
- DJs are personalities and curators — Scott Muni, Allison Steele "The Nightbird," Pete Fornatale, Vin Scelsa
- Album side identification — "side two of the new Pink Floyd"
- Long pauses, contemplative spaces — silence is allowed
- Heavy on the existential / philosophical occasionally
- Concert promotion central — promoters and venues advertise constantly
- Sponsor reads sometimes hip-aware ("for the head shop on the corner of...")

## Hot in the mid-70s (era texture for the test)

If targeting 1976-77:
- Fleetwood Mac, *Rumours* (1977)
- Hotel California / Eagles
- Pink Floyd, *Wish You Were Here* (1975), *Animals* (1977)
- Led Zeppelin still in catalog, *Presence* (1976)
- The Eagles, Linda Ronstadt, Jackson Browne, James Taylor — LA singer-songwriter wave
- Bruce Springsteen, *Born to Run* (1975) breaking nationally
- Bob Marley, *Exodus* (1977)
- Stevie Wonder, *Songs in the Key of Life* (1976)
- Steely Dan, *Aja* (1977)
- David Bowie, *Station to Station* (1976), *Low* / *Heroes* (1977 — the Berlin years)
- Punk emerging — Ramones debut (1976), Sex Pistols, Patti Smith *Horses* (1975)
- Hard rock — Aerosmith, KISS, Black Sabbath, Deep Purple
- Disco's commercial rise (largely shunned by FM rock DJs)

## Vocabulary

- "Far out"
- "Cosmic"
- "Heavy"
- "Mellow"
- "Right on"
- "The new one from..."
- "Side one, track three"
- "Up next..."
- "We've got [band] at [venue] tomorrow night, tickets still available"
- "This goes out to..."
- "Lay back," "kick back," "groove"
- "Maaan"
- "Catch this one"
- "An hour from now we'll be doing [theme block]"
- "Stay tuned"
- "Right after these words"

## Topics typical

- Vietnam aftermath / political commentary occasionally
- The energy crisis (1973, 1979)
- Watergate (1974)
- Concert listings (always concert listings)
- Drug references euphemized
- Album reviews / artist deep takes
- Listener call-ins for requests / dedications
- Charity fundraisers, marches, political organizing

## Test scenarios

Standard six, all of which fit this format naturally except possibly the genre-moment one, which adapts to "a deep cut introduction" or "an album-side announcement."

## DJ archetype for the cell

30s-40s, has been in radio since the 60s underground era, listened to the same albums as the audience, knows the bands personally from playing pre-fame, drinks beer with the local concert promoter, cynical about industry consolidation, plays the new Bowie even though management wants more Eagles. Voice is the texture as much as the words.
